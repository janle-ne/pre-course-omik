Chapter 1 · Foundations

What is Microeconomics?

Kinh tế vi mô là gì? — tại sao học nó?

Microeconomics (Kinh tế vi mô) is the branch of economics that studies the behavior of individual economic units — a single household, a firm, a worker, an investor — and the markets they form when they interact. The central question it asks is: how do individual agents make decisions under scarcity? (Kinh tế vi mô: nghiên cứu hành vi của các cá nhân, doanh nghiệp, thị trường — câu hỏi trọng tâm: con người ra quyết định thế nào khi nguồn lực có hạn?)

Macroeconomics, by contrast, deals with the whole economy as a single unit: national GDP, the overall price level (inflation), total unemployment, aggregate interest rates. Think of it this way: if you want to understand why your landlord raised your rent, that is micro. If you want to understand why prices across the whole country rose 5%, that is macro. (Kinh tế vĩ mô = cả nền kinh tế tổng hợp: GDP, lạm phát, thất nghiệp. Micro = từng đơn vị riêng lẻ.)

Two Core Concerns of Microeconomics · Hai mục tiêu cốt lõi
Lý thuyết ra quyết định hợp lý + mô tả hành vi trong điều kiện khan hiếm

1. Decision-Making Theory: It gives us a framework to explain rational choice — why agents prefer one option over another, and how they optimize given their constraints. This is positive (descriptive) theory: it says what people do, not what they should do.

2. Behavior Under Scarcity: Resources are limited — time, money, raw materials. Scarcity forces every agent to make trade-offs: producing more of one thing always means less of something else. Microeconomics models these trade-offs rigorously.

Real-life application: Why does a coffee shop charge more for a large cup than a small one, but not proportionally more? Why does a streaming service offer family plans at a discount? These pricing decisions are direct applications of micro principles.

Chapter 1 · Core Principles

The Seven Principles of Microeconomics

Bảy nguyên tắc nền tảng — nắm chắc phần này cho MCQ
1
People Face Trade-offs
Con người đối mặt với sự đánh đổi

To get anything you want, you must give up something else you also want. Resources — money, time, land — are finite, so choosing one thing always means forgoing another. The famous macro example is "guns vs. butter": a government that spends more on the military must spend less on healthcare or education. At a personal level: a student who spends Saturday studying gives up socializing. The trade-off that appears constantly in policy is Efficiency vs. Equality. Efficiency means society squeezes maximum output from its scarce resources. Equality means income and consumption are distributed fairly. Policies that redistribute income (taxes, welfare) reduce inequality but can shrink incentives to work and invest — making the economic pie smaller.

Ví dụ thực tế: Vietnam's government spending on a new highway means less budget for hospital upgrades. A company's investment in automation means fewer jobs for workers.

2
The Cost of Something Is What You Give Up to Get It — Opportunity Cost
Chi phí của một thứ là những gì bạn phải từ bỏ để có được nó — Chi phí cơ hội

Opportunity cost (chi phí cơ hội) is the value of the next best alternative you gave up when making a decision. It is one of the most important and most misunderstood concepts in economics — and a very common fill-in-the-blank target.

Crucially, opportunity cost includes both explicit costs (money actually paid) and implicit costs (foregone value of alternatives). Example: you decide to attend university instead of working. The explicit cost is tuition + books. But the implicit opportunity cost is the salary you could have earned if you had worked instead. Both count in the true economic cost of attending university. Example 2: going to see a movie costs the ticket price plus the value you place on the time you could have spent studying, working, or sleeping.

Ví dụ VN: Một sinh viên học đại học 4 năm không chỉ tốn học phí (chi phí tường minh) mà còn bỏ lỡ thu nhập 4 năm nếu đi làm (chi phí ngầm định = chi phí cơ hội thực sự).

3
Rational People Think at the Margin
Người hợp lý suy nghĩ theo biên — so sánh lợi ích biên và chi phí biên

Rational agents don't compare total costs to total benefits — they compare marginal (incremental) costs to marginal benefits. The word marginal always means "at the edge" or "one additional unit." If the marginal benefit of an action exceeds its marginal cost, a rational person does it. If the marginal cost exceeds the marginal benefit, a rational person does not.

Example: Should you study one more hour for tomorrow's exam? Don't ask "does studying at all benefit me?" — obviously yes. Ask: does one more hour of studying right now add more to your expected grade than the value you lose from one less hour of sleep? That marginal comparison is the right one.

Business application: A restaurant should stay open an extra hour if the revenue from the extra customers (marginal revenue) exceeds the wages of keeping staff longer (marginal cost). The accumulated sunk costs of setting up the restaurant are irrelevant to this marginal decision.

4
People Respond to Incentives
Con người phản ứng với các ưu đãi — thay đổi chi phí/lợi ích → thay đổi hành vi

An incentive is anything that alters the cost or benefit of an action, thus influencing behavior. Incentives can be positive (reward) or negative (penalty). When gasoline prices rise sharply, people drive less, buy more fuel-efficient cars, and use public transport more — they respond to the incentive created by higher price. When the government imposes a cigarette tax, the price of smoking rises — teen smoking falls because the marginal cost of the habit has increased.

Policy design must always anticipate behavioral responses. A government that sets a price ceiling on apartments intends to make housing affordable — but the incentive effect is that landlords reduce maintenance and new construction falls, eventually making the housing shortage worse.

Ví dụ VN: Khi chính phủ tăng thuế xăng, người dân mua xe điện hoặc đi xe máy điện nhiều hơn. Khi lãi suất ngân hàng tăng, hộ gia đình tiết kiệm nhiều hơn thay vì chi tiêu.

5
Trade Can Make Everyone Better Off
Thương mại có thể làm cho tất cả mọi người đều hưởng lợi — lợi thế so sánh

Rather than each person or country producing everything they need independently, specialization and trade allow everyone to consume a greater variety at lower cost. The key economic concept here is comparative advantage: even if one party is better at producing everything than another, there are still gains from specialization in what each does relatively best. Vietnam exports garments and electronics and imports aircraft and pharmaceuticals — both countries gain from this trade, even if one is more developed.

6
Markets Are Usually a Good Way to Organize Economic Activity
Thị trường thường là cách tổ chức hoạt động kinh tế hiệu quả — "bàn tay vô hình"

Adam Smith's famous invisible hand (1776, The Wealth of Nations): millions of decentralized decisions by households and firms, each pursuing only their own self-interest, are guided by prices to promote overall economic well-being — without any central planner coordinating them. A higher price signals to producers that a good is valuable and worth producing more of; it also signals to consumers to economize. This dual signal coordinates production and consumption efficiently. The invisible hand works because prices simultaneously reflect the value of goods to buyers (willingness to pay) and the cost of production to sellers.

7
Governments Can Sometimes Improve Market Outcomes
Chính phủ đôi khi có thể cải thiện kết quả thị trường — thất bại thị trường

Markets fail in two main ways. First, externalities (ngoại tác): when production or consumption imposes costs or benefits on third parties who are not involved in the transaction (e.g., a factory polluting a river — the victims don't participate in the market for the factory's product, so the market doesn't account for their costs). Second, market power (quyền lực thị trường): when a single buyer or seller has enough influence to set prices above competitive levels (e.g., a monopoly). Governments also intervene to enforce property rights — without secure property rights, no one has an incentive to invest, create, or produce.

Ví dụ VN: Chính phủ đặt tiêu chuẩn khí thải cho nhà máy (xử lý ngoại tác tiêu cực). Luật chống độc quyền ngăn một công ty kiểm soát toàn bộ thị trường.

⚠ Exam Trap — MCQ / Fill-in-the-Blank

The exam often asks you to match a scenario to a principle, or fill in a key term. Remember: Principle 2 = Opportunity Cost (what you give up); Principle 3 = Marginal thinking (incremental decisions); Principle 7's two market failures = Externalities and Market Power. These are the highest-tested terms from Chapter 1.

Chapter 2 · Supply

The Supply Curve

Đường cung — cách vẽ, cách dịch chuyển, và tại sao dốc lên
What is a Supply Curve? · Đường cung là gì?
Quan hệ giữa giá và lượng mà nhà sản xuất sẵn sàng + có thể bán

The supply curve shows the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity that producers are willing and able to sell at each price, holding everything else constant. Written as: QS = QS(P).

It slopes upward because higher prices make production more profitable — covering higher marginal costs and rewarding more effort. A firm that sells coffee pays for beans, milk, labour, and rent. If coffee prices rise, producing more cups is worthwhile even as marginal costs rise.

Critical distinction: "Change in supply" means the entire curve shifts (caused by anything other than price: production costs, technology, input prices, number of sellers, expectations). "Change in quantity supplied" means movement along the existing curve in response to a price change only.

Shifters of Supply (what moves the whole curve): (Các yếu tố dịch chuyển đường cung)

Lower input costs (cheaper raw materials, lower wages, cheaper energy) → supply increases → curve shifts right. Improved technology → can produce more with same inputs → curve shifts right. More producers enter the market → total market supply increases → curve shifts right. Reverse for the opposite changes.

📐 How to Draw the Supply Curve · Cách vẽ đường cung
1Draw two axes: vertical axis = Price (P), horizontal axis = Quantity (Q). Label them clearly.
2Draw a line from lower-left to upper-right (upward slope) — this is the supply curve S. Label it "S" at the end of the line.
3To show a price change: pick two points on S. Mark them as (P₁, Q₁) and (P₂, Q₂) with P₂ > P₁, Q₂ > Q₁. Draw dashed lines from each point to both axes. This is "movement along the curve."
4To show a supply increase: draw a second line S′ parallel to S but shifted to the right. The same price now corresponds to a larger quantity. Label S′. Add an arrow between S and S′ with the cause (e.g., "Cost ↓" or "Technology ↑").
5To show a supply decrease: draw S′′ shifted to the left (e.g., "Input cost ↑").

Đường S dốc lên. Dịch phải (S′) = cung tăng (giá thấp hơn / công nghệ tốt hơn). Dịch trái (S′′) = cung giảm. Di chuyển dọc đường = thay đổi lượng cung do giá thay đổi.

Chapter 2 · Demand

The Demand Curve

Đường cầu — luật cầu, hàng thay thế, hàng bổ sung
The Law of Demand · Luật cầu
Giá tăng → lượng cầu giảm — đường cầu dốc xuống

The demand curve shows the quantity consumers are willing to buy at each price: QD = QD(P). It slopes downward because of two reinforcing reasons: (1) Substitution reason — when the price of a good rises, it becomes more expensive relative to substitutes, so consumers switch to alternatives. (2) Income reason — a higher price reduces real purchasing power, so consumers can afford less of everything.

Shifters of Demand (non-price factors that move the whole curve): Income (higher income → more of normal goods); prices of related goods (substitutes and complements); tastes and preferences; number of buyers; expectations about future prices.

Substitute goods (hàng thay thế): two goods where an increase in the price of one causes demand for the other to rise. Pepsi and Coke, bus tickets and train tickets, tea and coffee. If the price of Coke rises, more people buy Pepsi.

Complementary goods (hàng bổ sung): two goods typically consumed together. If the price of one rises, demand for the other falls. Petrol and cars, smartphones and mobile data plans, coffee and milk. If petrol prices double, fewer people drive → demand for cars falls.

📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế

Bubble tea in Vietnam: When the price of milk tea rises from 35,000đ to 55,000đ, many students switch to fruit juice (substitute) — lượng cầu milk tea giảm, lượng cầu nước ép tăng. Meanwhile, if a new generation of young consumers grows up in Vietnam (more buyers in the market), the entire demand curve for milk tea shifts right even at the same price.

Smartphone and mobile data: When 5G phones become cheaper, demand for 5G data plans rises automatically — complementary goods. Cặp hàng bổ sung: giá điện thoại giảm → cầu data tăng.

📐 How to Draw the Demand Curve and Its Shifts · Cách vẽ đường cầu
1Same axes: P vertical, Q horizontal.
2Draw a downward-sloping line from upper-left to lower-right. Label it "D".
3Pick a price P₁. Draw a horizontal dashed line from P₁ to the demand curve. Drop a vertical dashed line to the Q-axis. Label that quantity Q₁. This reads: "at price P₁, quantity demanded = Q₁."
4To show demand increases (income rises, or a substitute's price rises): draw D′ parallel to D but to the right. At the same P₁, Q₂ > Q₁. Arrow from D to D′, label the cause.
5To show demand decreases: draw D′′ to the left.

Đường D dốc xuống. Tại cùng P₁: sau khi thu nhập tăng, lượng cầu tăng từ Q₁ lên Q₂ — đường dịch sang phải thành D′.

Chapter 2 · Equilibrium

Market Equilibrium and the Market Mechanism

Cân bằng thị trường — cơ chế tự điều chỉnh — thặng dư và thiếu hụt
Equilibrium Price P* · Giá cân bằng
Giá tại đó lượng cung = lượng cầu — thị trường "giải phóng"

The equilibrium (market-clearing) price P* is the price at which QS = QD. At this price, every buyer who wants to purchase the good at P* can find a seller, and every seller who wants to sell at P* can find a buyer. The market "clears" — no unsatisfied buyers or sellers remain.

The market mechanism is the automatic, self-correcting process that drives price toward equilibrium:

If price > P* (above equilibrium): Surplus (thặng dư) — QS > QD. Sellers produce more than buyers want. Unsold inventory accumulates. Sellers are motivated to lower price to clear stock. Price falls toward P*.

If price < P* (below equilibrium): Shortage (thiếu hụt) — QD > QS. Buyers want more than producers supply. Buyers compete against each other, bidding price up. Price rises toward P*.

📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế — COVID masks in 2020

At the start of COVID-19, the demand for face masks surged dramatically (the demand curve shifted sharply right). At the old equilibrium price, there was a massive shortage — pharmacies ran out instantly. The market mechanism pushed prices up sharply (some sellers charged 10× the old price), which both reduced quantity demanded and incentivized producers to ramp up production — restoring equilibrium at a much higher price and higher quantity. This is the market mechanism in action. (Đây là cơ chế thị trường: thiếu hụt → giá tăng → sản xuất nhiều hơn → cân bằng mới.)

📐 How to Draw Market Equilibrium and Show Surpluses/Shortages
1Draw both S (upward) and D (downward) on the same axes. Label both curves.
2Find where they intersect. Mark this point E with coordinates (Q*, P*). Draw dashed lines from E to both axes to show Q* and P*.
3To show a surplus: draw a horizontal line at some price P₁ > P*. Mark QS(P₁) on S and QD(P₁) on D. The gap QS − QD is the surplus. Draw a double-headed arrow on the Q-axis showing this gap, labeled "Surplus."
4To show a shortage: draw a line at P₂ < P*. QD(P₂) − QS(P₂) = shortage. Label it similarly.
5Add downward arrows near the surplus price (price falls) and upward arrows near the shortage price (price rises) — showing the market mechanism.

E = điểm cân bằng. Trên P*: thặng dư (S dư) → giá giảm về P*. Dưới P*: thiếu hụt (D dư) → giá tăng về P*. Thị trường tự cân bằng.

Chapter 2 · Elasticity

Elasticities of Supply and Demand

Độ co giãn — đo mức độ nhạy cảm của cung/cầu với giá và thu nhập

An elasticity (độ co giãn) measures the percentage change in one variable resulting from a 1-percent increase in another. It removes units, making it comparable across all goods and markets. Think of it as a "sensitivity score."

Price Elasticity of Demand Ep · Độ co giãn giá của cầu
Lượng cầu nhạy cảm thế nào với giá? — phần trăm thay đổi lượng cầu khi giá tăng 1%

Formula:

Ep = (%ΔQD) / (%ΔP) = (ΔQ/Q) / (ΔP/P) = (P/Q) · (ΔQ/ΔP)

Since demand curves slope downward, Ep is always negative. We typically discuss its magnitude:

|Ep|TypeVietnameseMeaningReal Example
> 1Elastic demandCầu co giãnQ falls more than proportionally when P risesLuxury handbags, holidays abroad
= 1Unit elasticCầu co giãn đơn vịQ falls exactly proportionallyTheoretical midpoint of linear curve
< 1Inelastic demandCầu kém co giãnQ falls less than proportionallyInsulin for diabetics, salt, cigarettes
= 0Perfectly inelasticCầu hoàn toàn kém co giãnQ does not change at all with priceLife-saving medicine (short run)
= ∞Perfectly elasticCầu hoàn toàn co giãnAny price rise causes Q to fall to zeroFirm in perfect competition
⚠ Classic Exam Trap — Slope ≠ Elasticity

On a linear demand curve Q = a − bP, the slope (−b) is constant everywhere. But elasticity changes at every single point. At the top of the curve (high P, low Q), the ratio P/Q is large, so |Ep| > 1 (elastic). At the bottom (low P, high Q), P/Q is small, so |Ep| < 1 (inelastic). The midpoint is unit elastic. A steeper demand curve does NOT automatically mean less elastic at every price — it depends on where on the curve you evaluate. (Bẫy thi: đường dốc hơn ≠ kém co giãn hơn tại mọi điểm. Elasticity = độ dốc × tỷ lệ P/Q.)

Income Elasticity EI · Độ co giãn thu nhập
Lượng cầu thay đổi bao nhiêu % khi thu nhập tăng 1%?

EI = (%ΔQD) / (%ΔI). If positive → normal good (hàng thông thường): demand rises with income. If negative → inferior good (hàng thứ cấp): demand falls as income rises. Example: instant noodles (mì tôm) — when income is low, you eat a lot of them; as your income rises, you switch to restaurant meals and eat fewer instant noodles. EI < 0 for instant noodles.

Cross-Price Elasticity EAB · Độ co giãn chéo
Cầu hàng A thay đổi bao nhiêu khi giá hàng B tăng 1%?

EAB = (%ΔQA) / (%ΔPB). Positive → substitutes (when PB rises, people buy more A). Negative → complements (when PB rises, people buy less A too). Zero → unrelated goods. This directly tests your understanding of substitutes and complements.

Short-Run vs. Long-Run Elasticity · Ngắn hạn vs. Dài hạn
Tại sao elasticity thay đổi theo thời gian?

Elasticities change because consumers and producers need time to adjust. For gasoline demand: short-run elasticity is very small (you can't immediately replace your car or change your commute) but large in the long run (people buy fuel-efficient cars, move closer to work). For automobile demand: the opposite — short-run elasticity is large because people can delay buying a new car when prices spike, but long-run elasticity is smaller because old cars eventually wear out and must be replaced regardless of price.

For supply: most goods have more elastic supply in the long run because producers have time to build new factories, train workers, and expand capacity. Exception: recycled (secondary) copper — long-run supply is less elastic than short-run because the stock of available scrap metal is finite and depletes over time.

Chapter 2 · Government Intervention

Price Controls

Kiểm soát giá — giá trần, giá sàn và hệ quả
Price Ceiling (Giá trần) vs. Price Floor (Giá sàn)
Giá trần = giá tối đa; Giá sàn = giá tối thiểu

A price ceiling (giá trần) is a legally imposed maximum price. It only has an economic effect if it is set below equilibrium P*. At Pmax < P*: quantity supplied falls, quantity demanded rises → shortage (QD − QS). The market cannot clear through the price mechanism, so goods are rationed by queues, connections, or other non-price methods. Example: apartment rent controls, which cause housing shortages over time.

A price floor (giá sàn) is a legally imposed minimum price. It only has an effect if set above equilibrium. At Pmin > P*: quantity supplied rises, quantity demanded falls → surplus (QS − QD). Classic example: minimum wage — at a wage floor above equilibrium, firms want to hire fewer workers than are willing to work → unemployment.

📌 Numerical Example · Ví dụ số liệu

Natural gas: Free-market price P* = $6.40/mcf. Government sets ceiling PG = $3.00. Supply equation gives QS = 20.6 Tcf; Demand equation gives QD = 29.1 Tcf. Shortage = 29.1 − 20.6 = 8.5 Tcf. This is a real case from 1970s US energy regulation. (Giá trần $3 thay vì $6.40 → thiếu hụt 8.5 Tcf khí đốt tự nhiên.)

⚠ Exam Trap — Ceiling vs. Floor

A ceiling BELOW equilibrium → shortage. A floor ABOVE equilibrium → surplus. If the ceiling is above P* or the floor is below P*, they have NO economic effect (the market already satisfies the constraint). This distinction appears in both MCQ and fill-in-the-blank. (Bẫy thi: giá trần chỉ hiệu quả khi NẰM DƯỚI P*. Giá sàn chỉ hiệu quả khi NẰM TRÊN P*.)

Chapter 3 · Budget Constraints

The Budget Constraint and Budget Line

Ràng buộc ngân sách và đường ngân sách

The theory of consumer behavior explains how a rational consumer allocates limited income to maximize well-being. It is built on three ordered steps: (1) Preferences — what does the consumer want? (2) Budget Constraints — what can they afford? (3) Consumer Choice — the optimal combination of both. Understanding each step separately before combining them is the key to clarity in this section.

The Budget Line Equation · Phương trình đường ngân sách
Tổng chi tiêu = thu nhập — tập hợp tất cả rổ hàng hóa vừa đủ ngân sách

If a consumer has income I, and buys two goods — Food (F) at price PF and Clothing (C) at price PC — the budget line is all baskets where all income is spent:

PF · F + PC · C = I

Rearranging to find the clothing intercept form: C = I/PC − (PF/PC) · F. The y-intercept (all income on C) is I/PC. The x-intercept (all income on F) is I/PF. The slope of the budget line = −PF/PC — the relative price of F in terms of C. It tells you how much C you must give up to buy one more unit of F.

Economic Meaning of Slope · Ý nghĩa kinh tế của độ dốc
Độ dốc = giá tương đối = chi phí cơ hội của một đơn vị hàng F tính theo hàng C

If PF = 20,000đ and PC = 40,000đ, the slope = −20,000/40,000 = −0.5. This means: to buy 1 more unit of F, you must give up 0.5 units of C. The ratio of prices is the opportunity cost of one good in terms of the other. (Ví dụ: phở 20k, áo 40k → mua thêm 1 tô phở = từ bỏ 0.5 cái áo.)

Two Key Rules about the Budget Line:

Rule 1 — Income change: If I changes (prices constant), the budget line shifts parallel outward (income rises) or inward (income falls). The slope does not change because relative prices haven't changed. (Thu nhập thay đổi → dịch song song, không thay đổi độ dốc.)

Rule 2 — Price change: If the price of one good changes (income and other price constant), the budget line rotates around the intercept of the unchanged good. If PF falls, the x-intercept (I/PF) increases — the line pivots outward on the F-axis. The y-intercept stays fixed because the price of C hasn't changed. (Giá một hàng thay đổi → xoay quanh điểm chặn của hàng kia.)

📐 How to Draw the Budget Line and Its Changes · Cách vẽ đường ngân sách
1Axes: vertical = Clothing (C), horizontal = Food (F). Both start from the origin.
2Mark the y-intercept at (0, I/PC) and the x-intercept at (I/PF, 0). Connect these two points with a straight line. This is your budget line L₁.
3For income increase: shift both intercepts proportionally outward → new line L₂ parallel to L₁.
4For PF decrease: y-intercept stays at (0, I/PC). Move only the x-intercept rightward to (I/PF,new, 0). The line rotates outward at the F-axis end → line L₃ (flatter slope).
5Baskets on the line = exactly affordable. Baskets below = affordable with money left over. Baskets above = unaffordable.
📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế

A student has 400,000đ/week budget (I = 400k). Phở = 40k (PF = 40k), Trà sữa = 50k (PC = 50k). Budget line: 40F + 50C = 400. Y-intercept: 8 cups of trà sữa. X-intercept: 10 bowls of phở. If the student gets extra 100k from parents, both intercepts increase proportionally — parallel shift outward. If phở price rises to 50k: y-intercept stays at 8, x-intercept falls to 8 → line rotates inward on the F-axis. (Trà sữa tăng giá → xoay vào phía trục C; thu nhập tăng → dịch ra ngoài song song.)

Chapter 3 · Preferences

Consumer Preferences and Indifference Curves

Sở thích người tiêu dùng — ba tiên đề và đường bàng quan

To compare baskets without directly measuring happiness, economists use three foundational axioms of preference:

Axiom 1: Completeness · Tính đầy đủ
Người tiêu dùng luôn có thể so sánh và xếp hạng bất kỳ hai rổ hàng nào

For any two baskets A and B, the consumer can always say: "I prefer A to B," "I prefer B to A," or "I am indifferent between A and B." No basket is "too weird to evaluate." This means a ranking always exists.

Axiom 2: Transitivity · Tính bắc cầu
Nếu A được ưa hơn B, và B được ưa hơn C, thì A phải được ưa hơn C

If A ≻ B and B ≻ C, then A ≻ C. This is logical consistency — without it, a consumer could be caught in a circular preference loop (prefer A over B, B over C, but C over A), which would make rational choice impossible. Transitivity rules this out. This axiom is what makes indifference curves unable to intersect (more on this below).

Axiom 3: Non-satiation (More is Better) · Tính không bão hòa
Người tiêu dùng luôn thích có nhiều hàng hóa hơn ít — chưa bao giờ "no đủ"

Goods are always desirable — having more of any good is always at least weakly better. This rules out satiation within the model. (In reality you might get sick from too much candy, but the model assumes we are always in the range where more is better.) This axiom means baskets with more of all goods are always preferred, and indifference curves slope downward (you have to give up some of Y to get more of X and stay at the same utility level).

Indifference Curve · Đường bàng quan
Tập hợp tất cả rổ hàng cho người tiêu dùng cùng một mức thỏa dụng

An indifference curve shows all combinations of two goods that give the consumer exactly the same level of satisfaction. The consumer is indifferent (equally happy) between any two baskets on the same curve.

An indifference map is a family of indifference curves. Higher curves (further from origin, toward upper-right) represent higher utility. Moving from curve U₁ to U₂ to U₃ means increasing well-being.

Why indifference curves cannot intersect: Suppose U₁ and U₂ cross at point A. Then basket B (on U₁ only) and basket D (on U₂ only) are both indifferent to A, meaning B ~ A and D ~ A. By transitivity, B ~ D. But if B has more of both goods than D, then B ≻ D by non-satiation — a contradiction. Therefore indifference curves can never intersect. (Nếu hai đường bàng quan giao nhau: dùng tiên đề bắc cầu + không bão hòa → mâu thuẫn → không thể xảy ra.)

📐 How to Draw an Indifference Map · Cách vẽ bản đồ bàng quan
1Axes: Y (Good Y) vertical, X (Good X) horizontal.
2Draw a curve that is convex to the origin (bowed inward) — it looks like the inside of a bowl. This is U₁. Label it at the right end.
3Draw a second curve further to the upper-right, same shape, labeled U₂. Then a third U₃ even further. These curves cannot touch or cross each other.
4Add an arrow pointing northeast labeled "Utility increases ↗" to show that higher curves = more satisfaction.
5Mark a point on U₂. Draw dashed lines to both axes to show the basket (X₀, Y₀). Any other point on U₂ gives the same utility.
Chapter 3 · MRS

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)

Tỷ lệ thay thế biên — đo sự sẵn sàng đánh đổi tại biên
Definition of MRS · Định nghĩa MRS
Lượng hàng Y tối đa người tiêu dùng sẵn sàng từ bỏ để được thêm 1 đơn vị X mà không thay đổi thỏa dụng

MRS is the maximum amount of good Y the consumer is willing to give up to obtain one additional unit of good X, while remaining on the same indifference curve (same utility level). It equals the absolute value of the slope of the indifference curve at that point. MRS is a local measure — it can change at every point along the curve.

Diminishing MRS · Tỷ lệ thay thế biên giảm dần
Khi có nhiều X hơn và ít Y hơn, ta sẵn sàng đánh đổi ít Y hơn cho thêm X

As we move down along an indifference curve (consuming more X, less Y), MRS falls. Why? Because as you accumulate more X, each additional unit of X becomes less valuable at the margin (diminishing marginal value). Simultaneously, as you give up Y, each remaining unit of Y becomes more precious. So you are willing to sacrifice less and less Y for each additional unit of X.

Real example: In your first hour of the day, you might trade 3 cups of coffee for 1 tea. But after your 5th cup of coffee (now you have a lot of coffee), you'd only trade 0.5 cups of coffee for 1 tea — coffee has become abundant, tea is now more desired. This is diminishing MRS. It is the reason indifference curves are convex to the origin (bowed inward). (Ví dụ: ban đầu có nhiều tiền và ít thời gian → sẵn sàng trả nhiều tiền để mua thêm thời gian. Khi đã có nhiều thời gian và ít tiền: sẵn sàng từ bỏ ít tiền hơn để có thêm 1 giờ.)

Perfect Substitutes & Perfect Complements · Hàng thay thế hoàn hảo và hàng bổ sung hoàn hảo
Hai trường hợp đặc biệt — đường bàng quan thẳng và chữ L

Perfect Substitutes: The consumer is always willing to trade the two goods at a constant ratio, regardless of how much of each is held. MRS is constant everywhere. Indifference curves are straight lines. Example: two brands of bottled water that taste identical — you'd always trade 1 Evian for 1 Aquafina, no matter how many of each you have.

Perfect Complements: The two goods must always be consumed in a fixed ratio. Having extra of one without the other adds zero utility. Indifference curves are L-shaped right angles. Example: left shoe and right shoe — a 5th left shoe with only 4 right shoes gives no extra utility. Both shoes together increase utility; either alone (in excess) does not.

⚠ Exam Trap — MRS and Convexity

MRS is always measured as a positive number (even though the slope of the indifference curve is negative). MRS = |ΔY/ΔX| = MUX/MUY. The fact that MRS decreases as we move along the curve (diminishing MRS) is the mathematical reason indifference curves are convex to the origin, not concave. If MRS were constant → straight line (perfect substitutes). If MRS were increasing → concave curve (very unusual, violates the standard assumptions). (MRS giảm dần → đường bàng quan lồi. Đây là tính chất tiêu chuẩn.)

Chapter 3 · Utility

Utility Functions and Marginal Utility

Hàm thỏa dụng và thỏa dụng biên — nền tảng toán học của lý thuyết người tiêu dùng
Utility Function · Hàm thỏa dụng
Hàm số gán con số đại diện cho mức thỏa mãn của từng rổ hàng

A utility function U(X, Y) assigns a number to each basket representing the consumer's satisfaction. Higher number = higher indifference curve = more utility. Example: U(F, C) = F × C. For basket (4, 5): U = 20. For basket (10, 2): U = 20. These two baskets give the same utility — they are on the same indifference curve.

Ordinal not Cardinal: The absolute numbers don't matter — only the ranking. U = 20 is not "twice as good" as U = 10 in any measurable sense. It just means the consumer prefers the U = 20 basket to the U = 10 basket. This is why we call it ordinal utility (thỏa dụng thứ bậc). (Chỉ thứ tự quan trọng, không phải giá trị tuyệt đối. Rổ A cho U=100 không "hạnh phúc gấp đôi" so với rổ B cho U=50 — chỉ có nghĩa A được ưa hơn B.)

Marginal Utility (MU) · Thỏa dụng biên
Mức tăng thỏa dụng khi tiêu thụ thêm 1 đơn vị một hàng, giữ nguyên mọi thứ khác

MUX = ∂U/∂X = additional utility from consuming one more unit of X, holding Y constant. MUY = ∂U/∂Y similarly.

Diminishing Marginal Utility: As more of a good is consumed, MU from each additional unit eventually falls. The first bowl of phở when you're starving is intensely satisfying. The second bowl is still good. By the 5th bowl, you're barely gaining extra satisfaction. This is diminishing marginal utility (thỏa dụng biên giảm dần). It is why indifference curves are convex and why risk-averse people dislike uncertainty.

MRS = MUX / MUY

This is a crucial formula. As X becomes more abundant, MUX falls. As Y becomes scarcer, MUY rises. Therefore MRS = MUX/MUY decreases as we move along the indifference curve — this is the mathematical foundation of diminishing MRS and convexity. (X nhiều hơn → MUX giảm; Y ít hơn → MUY tăng → MRS = MUX/MUY giảm → đường bàng quan lồi.)

Chapter 3 · Optimal Choice

The Consumer Optimum — Tangency Condition

Điểm tiêu dùng tối ưu — điều kiện tiếp tuyến và "bang per buck"

The utility-maximizing basket must satisfy two conditions simultaneously: (1) it lies on the budget line (all income is spent — rational consumers don't leave money unspent in a one-period model); (2) it achieves the highest possible indifference curve that is still reachable given the budget.

These two conditions are met exactly at the point where the budget line is tangent to an indifference curve — the only point where the indifference curve just touches the budget line without crossing it. At any other affordable point, a higher indifference curve is still reachable.

At optimum: MRS = PX / PY equivalently: MUX/PX = MUY/PY
The "Bang Per Buck" Condition · Điều kiện "thỏa dụng trên mỗi đồng"
Tại điểm tối ưu: thỏa dụng biên trên mỗi đồng chi tiêu bằng nhau cho tất cả hàng hóa

MUX/PX = MUY/PY means the marginal utility per dollar spent must be equal across all goods. If MUX/PX > MUY/PY, the consumer gets more satisfaction per dollar from X than from Y — so they should shift spending from Y to X until the ratio equalizes.

Real example: You're deciding between a coffee (20k, MU = 80 utils) and a tea (15k, MU = 45 utils). MU/P for coffee = 80/20 = 4. MU/P for tea = 45/15 = 3. Since 4 > 3, buy more coffee (and less tea) until the ratios equalize. At the optimum, both give the same MU per dong. (Tại điểm tối ưu: mỗi đồng chi tiêu cho bất kỳ hàng nào cũng mang lại thỏa dụng bằng nhau. Nếu cà phê mang lại nhiều hơn/đồng → dịch tiêu dùng sang cà phê.)

📐 How to Draw the Consumer Optimum · Cách vẽ điểm tối ưu
1Draw the budget line from (0, I/PY) to (I/PX, 0). Label it.
2Draw three indifference curves: U₁ (below-left of budget line — touches but doesn't use all the budget), U₂ (tangent to the budget line at one point — this is the optimum), U₃ (above-right of budget line — unattainable).
3Mark the tangency point E* where U₂ just touches the budget line. Draw dashed lines to both axes to show (X*, Y*).
4Label: "Slope of BL = −PX/PY" and "Slope of IC = −MRS" — at E*, these are equal.
5Note: U₁ is achievable but not optimal (can do better). U₃ is desired but not affordable. U₂ is the best achievable — the optimum.
Corner Solution · Nghiệm góc
Tối ưu tại biên — tiêu dùng bằng 0 một hàng hóa

A corner solution occurs when the optimal basket involves consuming zero of one good. This happens when the indifference curves are so steep (or flat) that the tangency point would be outside the feasible range. At the corner on the Y-axis: the consumer spends all income on Y and buys zero X. The MRS condition doesn't hold as an equality — instead, the marginal value of X is so low relative to its price that even one unit isn't worth buying. Example: a vegetarian would choose zero meat even if meat is very cheap.

Chapter 3 · Income & Price Changes

Income-Consumption Curve, Price-Consumption Curve, and Engel Curves

Đường tiêu dùng-thu nhập, đường tiêu dùng-giá, đường Engel
Income-Consumption Curve (ICC) · Đường tiêu dùng-thu nhập
Quỹ đạo các điểm tối ưu khi thu nhập thay đổi, giá không đổi

When income increases (prices fixed), the budget line shifts outward parallel. Each new budget line has a new optimum tangency point. Connecting all these optimum points as income varies traces the Income-Consumption Curve (ICC).

For a normal good: ICC slopes upward and to the right — as income rises, the consumer buys more of both goods. For an inferior good: as income rises beyond a threshold, the consumer buys less of that good — the ICC bends backward for that good. Classic example: instant noodles (mì gói rẻ tiền). At low incomes: buy lots of instant noodles. As income rises: switch to better food, instant noodle purchases fall. (ICC dốc lên phải = hàng thông thường. ICC uốn lại = hàng thứ cấp.)

Engel Curve · Đường Engel
Quan hệ trực tiếp giữa thu nhập và lượng tiêu dùng một hàng

An Engel curve plots the quantity of a specific good on the horizontal axis against income on the vertical axis, holding prices constant. For a normal good: Engel curve slopes upward (more income → more consumption). For an inferior good: Engel curve eventually bends backward (more income → less consumption). Ernst Engel observed historically that poorer households spend a larger share of income on food — this is Engel's Law. (Đường Engel dốc lên = hàng thông thường. Uốn lùi = hàng thứ cấp.)

Price-Consumption Curve (PCC) · Đường tiêu dùng-giá
Quỹ đạo các điểm tối ưu khi giá một hàng thay đổi

When the price of one good falls (income and other price fixed), the budget line rotates outward. Tracing the optimal baskets at each price level gives the Price-Consumption Curve (PCC). By reading off the quantity of that good at each price from the PCC, we derive the individual's demand curve — this is exactly how the demand curve is built from consumer theory. (Từ PCC → suy ra đường cầu cá nhân: mỗi điểm trên PCC là một điểm trên đường cầu.)

Chapter 3 · Decomposition of Price Effects

Income Effect and Substitution Effect

Hiệu ứng thu nhập và hiệu ứng thay thế — phân tách tác động của thay đổi giá

When the price of a good falls, two distinct forces act on the consumer simultaneously. Decomposing them is one of the most important analytical tools in consumer theory — and a favorite exam topic.

Substitution Effect · Hiệu ứng thay thế
Hàng trở nên rẻ tương đối → thay thế sang dùng nhiều hàng đó hơn, giữ nguyên thỏa dụng

The good's price has fallen relative to other goods — it is now relatively cheaper. Holding utility constant (staying on the same indifference curve), the consumer substitutes toward the now-relatively-cheaper good. The substitution effect is always in the opposite direction of the price change: price falls → substitution effect increases quantity demanded. This is true for all goods, always, without exception.

Graphically: slide along the original indifference curve U₁ to find the point tangent to a budget line with the new slope (new relative prices) but old utility level. The movement from the original optimum A to this new point D is the substitution effect.

Income Effect · Hiệu ứng thu nhập
Giá giảm → sức mua thực tế tăng → mua thêm hàng thông thường / mua ít hơn hàng thứ cấp

When price falls, real purchasing power increases — the consumer can now afford more with the same nominal income. This is like receiving a real income increase. For a normal good: higher real income → more consumption (income effect reinforces substitution effect). For an inferior good: higher real income → less consumption (income effect works against substitution effect).

Graphically: from point D (after substitution effect), shift to the new budget line (now at the new price) and find the new optimum B. Movement from D to B is the income effect.

Total Effect (A → B) = Substitution Effect (A → D) + Income Effect (D → B)
Good Type · Loại hàngSubstitution EffectIncome EffectTotal Effect on QD when P↓
Normal (Thông thường)↑ QD increases↑ QD also increases (real income ↑ = more normal good)↑↑ Strong increase — law of demand holds clearly
Inferior (Thứ cấp)↑ QD increases↓ QD decreases (real income ↑ = less inferior good)↑ Increases if substitution effect wins (usual case)
Giffen↑ QD increases↓↓ QD falls strongly (very large negative income effect)↓ DECREASES — price ↓ leads to QD ↓ (upward-sloping demand!)
Giffen Good · Hàng Giffen
Hàng thứ cấp mà hiệu ứng thu nhập âm lớn hơn hiệu ứng thay thế — đường cầu dốc lên

A Giffen good is a very special inferior good where the negative income effect is so large that it dominates the positive substitution effect. The result is paradoxical: when the price of a Giffen good falls, the quantity demanded also falls. This means the demand curve slopes upward — violating the normal law of demand.

The historical example: Robert Giffen allegedly observed that Irish peasants in the 19th century bought more potatoes when potato prices rose. Why? Potatoes were their main food staple (inferior good at low income). When potato prices rose, their real income fell so severely that they could no longer afford meat — they had to buy even more potatoes to survive, even at the higher price. When potato prices fell, their real income rose enough to afford some meat, so they bought fewer potatoes.

Why Giffen goods are extremely rare in practice: For the income effect to dominate, the good must (1) be a true inferior good, (2) account for a very large share of the consumer's budget, and (3) have no close affordable substitutes. These conditions almost never coexist. (Hàng Giffen cực kỳ hiếm trong thực tế. Điều kiện: phải là hàng thứ cấp + chiếm phần lớn ngân sách + không có hàng thay thế gần.)

Chapter 4 · Market Demand

From Individual Demand to Market Demand

Từ cầu cá nhân đến cầu thị trường — tổng hợp ngang
Horizontal Summation · Tổng hợp ngang
Tại mỗi mức giá: cộng lượng cầu của tất cả người tiêu dùng

The market demand curve is obtained by horizontally summing all individual demand curves. At each price level, add up the quantities demanded by every consumer. "Horizontal" summation means: fix P, add all Q's across consumers.

Example: At P = $4 per unit — Consumer A wants 0, B wants 4, C wants 7. Market quantity demanded = 0 + 4 + 7 = 11. At P = $1 — A wants 6, B wants 10, C wants 16. Market QD = 32. The resulting market demand curve is more elastic (flatter) than any individual demand curve because more consumers respond to price changes collectively.

Key insight: Any factor that changes one consumer's demand shifts the market demand curve. If a demographic group enters the market (population grows, new segment of buyers) → market demand shifts right. (Bất kỳ yếu tố nào làm thay đổi cầu cá nhân của nhiều người đồng thời → dịch chuyển đường cầu thị trường.)

📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế — Smartphone market Vietnam

Vietnam's smartphone market demand = sum of all individual consumer demands. As more rural consumers join the middle class (new buyers enter market), market demand shifts right even at the same price. When Samsung launches a new model that makes older Android phones cheaper, existing owners who delayed buying now buy → movement along the existing demand curve. (Người dùng mới gia nhập thị trường → đường cầu thị trường dịch phải. Giá điện thoại giảm → di chuyển dọc đường.)

Chapter 4 · Labor Supply

Work-Leisure Decisions and Labor Supply

Quyết định làm việc-nghỉ ngơi — cung lao động và hiệu ứng wage

Labor supply decisions are fundamentally decisions about how to allocate time. Each day has 24 fixed hours. The household must divide these between labor (earns wage income but sacrifices leisure) and leisure (direct utility but no income). This is a utility-maximization problem with a budget constraint — exactly like the two-good consumer problem, but with time as the resource.

The Two Constraints · Hai ràng buộc
Ràng buộc ngân sách + ràng buộc thời gian → ràng buộc ngân sách mở rộng

Budget constraint: Total consumption spending = wage income + non-labor income. If C = consumption good (price p), M = non-labor income (rent, dividends), w = wage rate, L = hours of labor: p·C = M + w·L.

Time constraint: L + F = T, where F = leisure hours and T = total available time (e.g., 16 waking hours). Labor hours + leisure hours = total time.

Combining these two constraints into one: p·C + w·F = M + w·T. This is the "full income" budget constraint — the wage rate w acts like the price of leisure (the opportunity cost of one leisure hour is the wage you give up). (Giá của "nghỉ ngơi" = mức lương w: mỗi giờ nghỉ ngơi tốn w đồng thu nhập bị bỏ lỡ.)

Effect of a Wage Increase · Tác động của tăng lương
Lương tăng: hiệu ứng thay thế và thu nhập hoạt động ngược chiều nhau

Substitution Effect of wage increase: A higher wage makes leisure more expensive (its opportunity cost rises). The consumer substitutes away from leisure toward work. More work → substitution effect of wage ↑ is to work more.

Income Effect of wage increase: A higher wage increases real income. At higher income, the consumer wants more of all normal goods, including leisure. So they want to work less to enjoy more leisure. Income effect of wage ↑ is to work less.

Net result: At low wages, the substitution effect tends to dominate → labor supply curve slopes upward (higher wage → more hours worked). At very high wages, the income effect may dominate → labor supply curve bends backward (higher wage → fewer hours, because you can afford to enjoy more leisure). This backward-bending labor supply curve is a well-documented real-world phenomenon. (Đường cung lao động: ban đầu dốc lên (hiệu ứng thay thế thắng); ở mức lương cao, có thể uốn ngược (hiệu ứng thu nhập thắng).)

📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế

A young worker in Vietnam earns 8M đồng/month and works 48 hours/week. Her employer offers a raise to 12M đồng/month. The substitution effect says: each hour of leisure now costs more (12M vs 8M effective hourly rate) → work more. But the income effect says: she's now richer, can afford to relax → work less. If she ends up working 50 hours, substitution effect dominated. If she negotiates to reduce to 40 hours/week, income effect dominated. Many high-earning professionals in wealthy countries work fewer hours than lower earners — the backward-bending portion. (Ở Việt Nam: nhiều công nhân mới tăng giờ làm khi lương tăng. Ở mức lương cao: luật sư, bác sĩ đôi khi làm ít giờ hơn khi giàu hơn.)

Chapter 4 · Intertemporal Decisions

Intertemporal Consumption: Saving vs. Spending Now

Quyết định tiêu dùng liên thời kỳ — tiêu hôm nay hay để dành?

Intertemporal decisions ask: how should a household allocate income across time? The simple two-period model uses: period 0 (present, C₀, income M₀) and period 1 (future, C₁, income M₁). The household can save in period 0 (earn interest) or borrow against future income.

The Intertemporal Budget Constraint · Ràng buộc ngân sách liên thời kỳ
Giá trị hiện tại của tiêu dùng = giá trị hiện tại của thu nhập

With interest rate r: saving S₀ = M₀ − C₀ in period 0 grows to (1+r)S₀ in period 1. So C₁ = M₁ + (1+r)(M₀ − C₀). Rearranging:

C₀ + C₁/(1+r) = M₀ + M₁/(1+r) ← Present Value Form

Left side: present value of lifetime consumption. Right side: present value of lifetime income (permanent income). The slope of this intertemporal budget line is −(1+r): giving up 1 unit of consumption today (saving) allows (1+r) units of future consumption. (Hy sinh 1 đơn vị tiêu dùng hôm nay → được (1+r) đơn vị ngày mai — tiết kiệm có lãi.)

Optimal Intertemporal Choice · Lựa chọn liên thời kỳ tối ưu
Điều kiện tối ưu: MRS giữa tiêu dùng hiện tại và tương lai = (1+r)

The optimum is where the intertemporal indifference curve (showing combinations of C₀ and C₁ with equal lifetime utility) is tangent to the intertemporal budget line. At this point:

MRS(C₀, C₁) = (1+r)

The marginal rate of substitution between present and future consumption equals the gross interest rate. People have a positive time preference rate ρ — they prefer to consume now rather than later (everything else equal). A higher interest rate (r) makes future consumption relatively cheaper → the consumer saves more (substitution effect of r) but is also richer (income effect). The net result of an interest rate change on savings is ambiguous in general. (Con người ưa tiêu dùng hiện tại. Lãi suất tăng: có thể tiết kiệm nhiều hơn (hiệu ứng thay thế) hoặc ít hơn (hiệu ứng thu nhập) — kết quả tổng hợp không xác định.)

📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế

You earn 10M đồng this month and expect to earn 12M next month. With bank interest rate r = 5%, your present-value budget: C₀ + C₁/1.05 = 10 + 12/1.05 ≈ 21.43M. If you value current and future consumption equally and have no time preference, you'd consume about 10.7M each period. But if you prefer current consumption strongly, you spend more now and less later. A rise in r from 5% to 8% makes future consumption relatively cheaper — you might save more (substitution effect) but if you're a net saver, you're richer so you also consume more now (income effect). (Lãi suất tăng: người tiết kiệm được lợi (thu nhập lãi tăng) nhưng tiêu dùng hiện tại cũng trở nên đắt tương đối hơn — tổng hợp không rõ ràng.)

Uncertainty & Consumer Behavior

Rủi ro · Xác suất · Thỏa dụng kỳ vọng · Thái độ với rủi ro · Giảm thiểu rủi ro
Chapter 5 · Describing Risk

Probability, Expected Value, and Variability

Xác suất, giá trị kỳ vọng, và độ biến động — ba công cụ mô tả rủi ro
Probability · Xác suất
Khả năng một kết quả xảy ra — khách quan hoặc chủ quan

Objective probability: Based on observed frequencies or symmetry. The probability of a fair coin landing heads = 0.5 (based on observed statistics over many tosses). The probability of a flood in a specific area = based on historical flood records.

Subjective probability: Personal judgment about likelihood, not based on objective data. Your personal belief about whether your new business will succeed. Different people may assign different subjective probabilities to the same event. (Xác suất khách quan = dựa trên dữ liệu thực tế. Xác suất chủ quan = nhận định cá nhân.)

Expected Value (EV) · Giá trị kỳ vọng
Bình quân gia quyền của tất cả kết quả có thể — đo xu hướng trung tâm

EV is the probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes. For n outcomes: E(X) = Pr₁·X₁ + Pr₂·X₂ + … + Prₙ·Xₙ. It tells you what outcome you would expect on average if you repeated the risky situation many times.

E(X) = Pr₁ · X₁ + Pr₂ · X₂ (two outcomes)
📌 Numerical Example · Ví dụ số liệu

Investment Decision: Stock A: 30% chance of earning 5M đồng, 70% chance of earning 2M đồng. EV(A) = 0.3 × 5 + 0.7 × 2 = 1.5 + 1.4 = 2.9M đồng. Stock B: 50% chance of 4M đồng, 50% chance of 1.8M đồng. EV(B) = 0.5 × 4 + 0.5 × 1.8 = 2 + 0.9 = 2.9M đồng. Both have the same expected value! But they differ in risk (variability). (Hai khoản đầu tư có EV bằng nhau nhưng rủi ro khác nhau — đây là lý do cần thêm công cụ đo variability.)

Variability: Variance and Standard Deviation · Độ biến động: phương sai và độ lệch chuẩn
Đo mức độ phân tán của kết quả — phương sai = bình quân bình phương độ lệch

Two jobs with the same EV = $1,500/month can have very different risk. Job 1 (commission): $2,000 with prob 0.5, $1,000 with prob 0.5. Job 2 (mostly fixed): $1,510 with prob 0.99, $510 with prob 0.01. Same EV = $1,500. But Job 1 has much higher variability — outcomes are far from the mean.

Variance = Pr₁·(X₁ − EV)² + Pr₂·(X₂ − EV)² · Standard Deviation = √Variance

For Job 1: Variance = 0.5·(2000−1500)² + 0.5·(1000−1500)² = 0.5·250,000 + 0.5·250,000 = 250,000. SD = $500. For Job 2: Variance = 0.99·(1510−1500)² + 0.01·(510−1500)² = 0.99·100 + 0.01·980,100 = 99 + 9,801 = 9,900. SD ≈ $99.50. Job 1 is riskier by a large margin despite identical expected income. (SD lớn = rủi ro cao hơn. Job 1 có SD=$500, Job 2 có SD=$99.50 — Job 2 an toàn hơn nhiều.)

Chapter 5 · Risk Preferences

Risk Aversion, Risk Neutrality, and Risk Loving

Ba thái độ đối với rủi ro — hàm thỏa dụng lõm, thẳng, lồi

To analyze choices under uncertainty, we measure payoffs in utility terms. Expected utility E(u) = Pr₁·u(X₁) + Pr₂·u(X₂) — the probability-weighted average of the utilities from each outcome. This is fundamentally different from the utility of the expected value u(EV). The relationship between E(u) and u(EV) determines attitudes toward risk.

Risk Averse · Ngại rủi ro
Thích thu nhập chắc chắn hơn thu nhập rủi ro có cùng giá trị kỳ vọng

A risk-averse person prefers a certain income of $1,500 to a 50/50 gamble between $2,000 and $1,000 (also expected value = $1,500). Why? Because of diminishing marginal utility of income. The utility gained from the extra $500 (going from $1,500 to $2,000) is less than the utility lost from losing $500 (going from $1,500 to $1,000). Therefore: E(u) < u(EV). The utility function is concave (lõm — bowed downward). Most people in most real situations are risk averse. (Ngại rủi ro: hàm thỏa dụng lõm. Mất $500 đau hơn được $500 vui. → Thích chắc chắn.)

Risk Neutral · Trung lập với rủi ro
Bàng quan giữa chắc chắn và rủi ro khi EV bằng nhau — hàm thỏa dụng tuyến tính

A risk-neutral person cares only about expected value — identical expected values produce identical utility, regardless of variance. E(u) = u(EV). Utility function is linear. Some financial investors or large corporations may behave approximately risk-neutrally when the stakes are small relative to their total wealth.

Risk Loving · Ưa rủi ro
Thích rủi ro hơn chắc chắn — hàm thỏa dụng lồi

A risk-loving person prefers the gamble to a certain income with the same expected value. E(u) > u(EV). Utility function is convex (lồi). Example: gamblers who play at unfavorable odds for the excitement — they derive utility from the thrill of uncertainty itself, not just the expected payoff. Also: people buying lottery tickets (objectively a losing proposition by expected value) exhibit risk-loving behavior for small stakes. (Ưa rủi ro: hàm thỏa dụng lồi. Hiếm gặp hơn ngại rủi ro trong các quyết định quan trọng.)

Risk Premium · Mức bù rủi ro (Phí rủi ro)
Số tiền tối đa người ngại rủi ro sẵn sàng trả để tránh rủi ro

The risk premium is the difference between the expected value of a risky prospect and the certainty equivalent (CE) — the certain income that gives the same expected utility as the gamble. Risk Premium = EV − CE. A more risk-averse person has a larger risk premium (they'd pay more to eliminate risk). This is the economic foundation of insurance premiums.

Example: EV = $1,500 but you'd be equally happy with a certain $1,300. Risk premium = $1,500 − $1,300 = $200. You'd pay up to $200 to convert the risky gamble into a certain $1,300. (Phần bù rủi ro = EV − CE. Người ngại rủi ro nhiều hơn → phần bù rủi ro lớn hơn → sẵn sàng trả bảo hiểm cao hơn.)

⚠ Exam Trap — E(u) vs. u(EV)

These are NOT the same! For a risk-averse person: E(u) < u(EV) — the utility of the gamble is less than the utility they'd get from the expected value delivered with certainty. This inequality defines risk aversion mathematically (concave utility function). (Thỏa dụng kỳ vọng E(u) ≠ thỏa dụng của giá trị kỳ vọng u(EV). Người ngại rủi ro: E(u) < u(EV).)

Chapter 5 · Risk Reduction

Reducing Risk: Diversification, Insurance, and the Law of Large Numbers

Giảm thiểu rủi ro: đa dạng hóa, bảo hiểm, và quy luật số lớn
Diversification · Đa dạng hóa
Phân bổ nguồn lực vào nhiều hoạt động không tương quan để giảm rủi ro tổng thể

Diversification reduces risk by spreading resources across activities whose outcomes are not positively correlated. If you sell only air conditioners, income is $30,000 in hot summers and $12,000 in cold winters (average $21,000, high risk). If you sell both air conditioners and heaters in equal amounts, income is approximately $21,000 regardless of weather — risk is eliminated because the two outcomes are negatively correlated (when one is good, the other is bad). When correlation is negative, diversification is most powerful. When correlation is positive (two ice cream brands in the same market — both suffer when it's cold), diversification provides no risk reduction. (Đa dạng hóa hiệu quả nhất khi các kết quả tương quan âm. Nếu tương quan dương: đa dạng hóa không giảm được rủi ro.)

Insurance · Bảo hiểm
Người ngại rủi ro sẵn sàng trả phí bảo hiểm "công bằng" để đổi lấy sự chắc chắn

A homeowner has wealth = $50,000. Probability of burglary = 10%, loss = $10,000. Without insurance: EV(wealth) = 0.9×$50,000 + 0.1×$40,000 = $49,000 with high risk (SD = $3,000). With fair insurance at premium = $1,000 (= 10% × $10,000): wealth = $49,000 with certainty (SD = 0). A risk-averse person prefers certainty at $49,000 to uncertainty with the same EV. They are willing to buy the insurance. (Bảo hiểm: trả phí = 1,000đ để đảm bảo luôn có 49,000đ dù có bị trộm hay không. Người ngại rủi ro thích vậy hơn.)

Law of Large Numbers · Quy luật số lớn
Khi mẫu đủ lớn: kết quả trung bình tiến đến giá trị kỳ vọng — nền tảng ngành bảo hiểm

While individual outcomes are unpredictable (will your specific house be burglarized?), the average outcome over many similar independent events converges to the expected value with high precision. An insurance company covering 10,000 similar homes can predict very accurately that approximately 10% = 1,000 will be burglarized. This predictability allows the company to set premiums, pool risk, and make insurance actuarially fair. Individual risk is replaced by predictable statistical averages at scale. (Công ty bảo hiểm: không biết nhà nào bị trộm, nhưng biết chắc khoảng 10% tổng số nhà bị trộm → lập phí bảo hiểm chính xác.)

A mutual fund applies the same logic to investment: by pooling funds from many small investors to buy a diversified portfolio, each investor's risk is reduced dramatically without sacrificing expected returns. An individual investor with 5M đồng cannot diversify across 50 stocks; a mutual fund with 500B đồng can. (Quỹ tương hỗ = đa dạng hóa cho nhà đầu tư nhỏ thông qua gộp vốn.)

📌 Real-Life Example · Ví dụ thực tế — VN stock market

A student has 20M đồng to invest. If she buys only one stock (e.g., VinGroup), she bears all the risk of that one company. If she buys shares in a Vietnamese index fund that holds VinGroup, Vietcombank, Vinamilk, FPT, and 96 other companies, her risk is dramatically reduced. A bad year for one company is partially offset by a good year for others. This is diversification via mutual funds. (Quỹ chỉ số nắm giữ nhiều cổ phiếu → rủi ro của mỗi cổ phiếu được phân tán. Nhà đầu tư nhỏ hưởng lợi từ luật số lớn.)

Production Decisions of a Firm

Chapter 6 · Production Theory

Factors of Production and the Production Function

Các yếu tố sản xuất và hàm sản xuất
Three Factors of Production · Ba yếu tố đầu vào sản xuất
Lao động (L), Vốn (K), Nguyên vật liệu — ba nguồn lực chuyển hóa thành đầu ra

Labor (L) · Lao động: All human effort — skilled workers, unskilled workers, managers. Time and brainpower.

Capital (K) · Vốn: Physical capital — buildings, machines, equipment, vehicles, computers. Also land. Note: in economics, "capital" means physical productive assets, NOT financial capital (money). Firms use financial capital to buy capital goods.

Materials · Nguyên vật liệu: Raw materials, energy, intermediate inputs (steel for an auto plant, flour for a bakery, electricity for a factory).

Production Function · Hàm sản xuất
q = F(K, L) — sản lượng tối đa đạt được từ mỗi tổ hợp (K, L) khi hoạt động hiệu quả

The production function q = F(K, L) shows the maximum output achievable from every specified combination of K and L, assuming the firm operates efficiently (no waste). It describes the technological frontier — what is physically possible. The actual output of an inefficient firm may be below this frontier, but the production function shows the maximum.

Example: a coffee shop's production function: q (cups of coffee per hour) = F(machines K, baristas L). With 2 machines and 2 baristas, maybe q = 40 cups/hour. With 2 machines and 4 baristas (doubling only labor), maybe q = 65 cups/hour. The production function captures this relationship precisely.

Short Run vs. Long Run · Ngắn hạn vs. Dài hạn
Ngắn hạn: ít nhất một yếu tố cố định. Dài hạn: tất cả yếu tố đều có thể thay đổi

Short run: At least one input is fixed — typically capital (K is fixed). The firm cannot immediately build a new factory or install new machinery. It can only adjust variable inputs like labor. A restaurant can hire more waiters tonight but cannot expand its dining room by tomorrow.

Long run: All inputs are variable. Given sufficient time, the firm can adjust everything — hire more workers, build new facilities, buy new machines, or exit the industry entirely. The long run is defined not by calendar time but by the ability to adjust all factors. (Ngắn hạn: K cố định, L biến đổi. Dài hạn: tất cả biến đổi. Không phải ngày/tháng mà là khả năng điều chỉnh.)

Chapter 6 · One Variable Input

Production with One Variable Input: Total, Average, and Marginal Products

Sản xuất với một đầu vào biến đổi — TP, AP, MP và quy luật hiệu suất biên giảm dần
Total Product (TP), Average Product (AP), and Marginal Product (MP)
Tổng sản lượng, năng suất bình quân, và năng suất biên

Total Product (TP): The total output q produced from given quantities of K and L. As L increases (K fixed), TP first rises at an increasing rate, then at a decreasing rate, reaches a maximum, and may eventually fall (if too many workers crowd into the fixed space).

Average Product of Labor (APL) = q / L: Output per worker. Like the "productivity" of the average employee.

Marginal Product of Labor (MPL) = Δq / ΔL: The additional output from hiring one more unit of labor, with K fixed. This is the most important concept — always think about the next worker's contribution.

Key Relationships between TP, AP, and MP · Quan hệ giữa TP, AP, và MP
Bốn quy tắc quan trọng — thường xuất hiện trong MCQ

Rule 1: When MP > AP, AP is rising. (The new worker is more productive than the average → average rises.)

Rule 2: When MP < AP, AP is falling. (The new worker is less productive than the average → average falls.)

Rule 3: MP = AP at the maximum of AP. (MP intersects AP at the peak of AP.) At this point, adding one more worker neither raises nor lowers the average productivity.

Rule 4: When TP is at its maximum, MP = 0. Beyond this point, additional workers actually reduce total output (negative MP) — they overcrowd or obstruct each other.

Real example: Pizza restaurant with a fixed oven (K). First worker (L=1): bakes 10 pizzas/hour (MP=10, TP=10). Second (L=2): now they can split tasks — one makes dough, one bakes. TP=24, MP=14, AP=12. Third (L=3): TP=30, MP=6 — still positive but falling. At L=6: TP starts falling (too crowded) → MP is negative. (L=2 có MP cao nhất. Sau đó MP giảm. Khi lò nướng quá đông: MP âm, TP giảm.)

📐 How to Draw TP, AP, and MP Curves · Cách vẽ đường TP, AP và MP
1Draw two panels vertically, one above the other. Top panel: TP vs. L. Bottom panel: AP and MP vs. L. Both share the same x-axis (L).
2In top panel: draw TP as an S-shaped curve (sigmoidal) — first rises steeply (increasing MP), then flattens (decreasing MP), reaches a peak, then slopes slightly downward (negative MP). Mark the peak as "Max TP (MP=0)".
3Mark an inflection point on TP (where it changes from concave to convex) — this is where MP is at its maximum. Draw a line from this point down to the x-axis labeling it "L at MP max."
4In the bottom panel: draw MP as an inverted U (rises, peaks at inflection of TP, then falls, crosses zero at peak of TP, then goes negative). Draw AP similarly but with a lower, flatter inverted U.
5Make MP cross AP exactly at the maximum of AP. This crossing point is critical — mark it clearly labeled "MP = AP (AP max)."
6Add a zero line in the bottom panel. MP crosses zero at the same L where TP reaches its maximum (these are vertically aligned).
Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns · Quy luật hiệu suất biên giảm dần
Thêm đầu vào biến đổi vào đầu vào cố định → MP cuối cùng giảm — quy luật ngắn hạn

As the use of a variable input (labor) increases while other inputs remain fixed, the resulting additions to output (marginal product) will eventually decrease. Two important qualifications: (1) MP may initially increase before eventually falling (the S-shape of the TP curve). (2) This is strictly a short-run concept — it applies when at least one input is fixed. Technology improvements shift the entire TP curve upward without violating this law.

Why it happens: The fixed factor (capital K) becomes increasingly scarce relative to the expanding variable factor (labor L). Each additional worker has less capital to work with, so their contribution falls. (Vốn cố định ngày càng khan hiếm hơn khi lao động tăng → mỗi lao động thêm có ít máy móc hơn để làm việc → đóng góp giảm.)

⚠ Critical Exam Trap — Short Run NOT Long Run

The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns applies only in the short run, when at least one input is fixed. In the long run (all inputs variable), this law does NOT apply — we use "Returns to Scale" instead. Confusing these two concepts (DMR vs. DRS) is the #1 error in Chapter 6. (DMR = ngắn hạn, một yếu tố cố định. Returns to Scale = dài hạn, tất cả yếu tố thay đổi. KHÔNG được nhầm lẫn.)

Chapter 6 · Two Variable Inputs

Isoquants and the Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution (MRTS)

Đường đẳng lượng và tỷ lệ thay thế kỹ thuật biên
Isoquant · Đường đẳng lượng
Tập hợp tất cả tổ hợp (K, L) cho cùng mức sản lượng — tương tự đường bàng quan

In the long run, both K and L are variable. An isoquant shows all combinations of capital and labor that produce the same level of output. It is the production-theory analog of the indifference curve in consumer theory. Higher isoquants = higher output levels.

Properties of isoquants (paralleling indifference curves): (1) Downward sloping — to produce the same output with less capital, you need more labor. (2) Convex to origin — MRTS diminishes. (3) Cannot intersect — same logical argument as for indifference curves. (4) Higher isoquant = more output.

MRTS — Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution · Tỷ lệ thay thế kỹ thuật biên
Tỷ lệ K có thể thay thế bằng L trong sản xuất mà không đổi sản lượng

MRTS = −ΔK/ΔL (keeping output constant) = the rate at which capital can be substituted by labor while maintaining the same output. Equivalently, MRTS = MPL/MPK.

MRTS = MPL / MPK = −(ΔK/ΔL) at constant output

Diminishing MRTS: As we move along an isoquant (using more L and less K), MRTS falls. This happens because as L increases, MPL falls (diminishing marginal returns to labor); as K decreases, MPK rises (capital becomes more scarce and more productive at the margin). So MRTS = MPL/MPK falls. This is the reason isoquants are convex to the origin — the production-side parallel of diminishing MRS in consumer theory. (L tăng → MPL giảm; K giảm → MPK tăng → MRTS = MPL/MPK giảm → đường đẳng lượng lồi về gốc.)

Special Isoquant Cases · Hai trường hợp đặc biệt
Thay thế hoàn hảo và tỷ lệ cố định trong sản xuất

Perfect substitutes in production: Isoquants are straight lines with constant MRTS. Capital and labor can always be traded at the same ratio regardless of amounts. Example: a simple digging task where one worker with a shovel can always be replaced by one worker with a slightly bigger shovel (or a robot can be replaced by proportional human labor at constant rate).

Fixed-proportions (Leontief production function): Isoquants are L-shaped right angles. Only one combination of K and L per unit of output is possible. Adding extra labor without more capital does nothing, and vice versa. Example: one truck + one driver = one delivery unit. Two trucks + one driver = still one delivery unit. Two trucks + two drivers = two delivery units. (Ví dụ VN: một xe máy giao hàng luôn cần một tài xế — tỷ lệ cố định hoàn hảo. Thêm tài xế mà không thêm xe: không giao được thêm hàng.)

📐 How to Draw an Isoquant Map · Cách vẽ bản đồ đường đẳng lượng
1Axes: K (vertical), L (horizontal). Both start from origin.
2Draw three convex-to-origin curves, each labeled with an output level (q₁, q₂, q₃) with q₃ > q₂ > q₁. Further from origin = more output.
3To show MRTS at a specific point: mark a point P on q₂. Draw a small right triangle: move ΔL to the right, then ΔK upward until you're back on q₂. MRTS = ΔK/ΔL at P = the absolute slope of the isoquant at P.
4Show that as you move rightward along the isoquant, the triangles get flatter (smaller ΔK for same ΔL) — this shows diminishing MRTS.
Chapter 6 · Returns to Scale

Returns to Scale

Hiệu suất theo quy mô — điều gì xảy ra khi tất cả đầu vào tăng cùng tỷ lệ?

Returns to scale is a long-run concept that describes what happens to output when all inputs are increased proportionately (e.g., doubled). It differs fundamentally from the law of diminishing marginal returns (short run, one input fixed).

How to test returns to scale from a production function: Replace K with tK and L with tL for any t > 1. If F(tK, tL) = t·q → Constant Returns to Scale. If F(tK, tL) > t·q → Increasing Returns to Scale. If F(tK, tL) < t·q → Decreasing Returns to Scale. (Kiểm tra: thay K→tK, L→tL. So sánh F(tK,tL) với t·F(K,L) để xác định loại hiệu suất.)

Increasing Returns to Scale (IRS) · Hiệu suất tăng theo quy mô
Tăng gấp đôi tất cả đầu vào → sản lượng tăng hơn gấp đôi

F(2K, 2L) > 2q. Why does IRS arise? (1) Specialization: at larger scale, workers can specialize in narrower tasks and become far more productive. A small bakery has one baker doing everything; a large bakery has separate specialists for each step. (2) Geometric properties: doubling all linear dimensions of a factory more than doubles floor space (area scales with the square, volume with the cube of linear dimensions). (3) Spreading fixed costs: R&D, brand building, and management systems have high fixed costs but serve larger production at near-zero marginal cost.

On an isoquant map: with IRS, isoquants are spaced more and more closely together as output increases (less doubling of inputs needed to double output). (IRS: đường đẳng lượng ngày càng gần nhau hơn khi quy mô tăng. Phổ biến trong công nghiệp chế tạo, công nghệ.)

Constant Returns to Scale (CRS) · Hiệu suất không đổi theo quy mô
Tăng gấp đôi đầu vào → sản lượng tăng đúng gấp đôi

F(2K, 2L) = 2q exactly. Output scales proportionally. Isoquants are equally spaced. CRS implies that average cost per unit is constant at all scales — there is no inherent advantage to being bigger or smaller. Many industries behave approximately as CRS over a range of output. Example: a chain of identical coffee shops — doubling the number of shops (K and L both double) doubles the number of cups sold. (CRS: đường đẳng lượng cách đều nhau. Chi phí bình quân không thay đổi theo quy mô.)

Decreasing Returns to Scale (DRS) · Hiệu suất giảm theo quy mô
Tăng gấp đôi đầu vào → sản lượng tăng ít hơn gấp đôi

F(2K, 2L) < 2q. Why? Primarily due to management diseconomies — very large organizations become increasingly difficult to coordinate and control. Communication costs rise, decision-making slows, and bureaucracy multiplies. Also: geographic constraints (a mine can only be expanded so deep before rock quality deteriorates), or specific natural resource constraints. On an isoquant map: isoquants are spaced further and further apart as output increases (need more than proportional input increases to raise output). (DRS: quy mô quá lớn → khó quản lý, phối hợp kém hiệu quả → sản lượng tăng ít hơn đầu vào. Đường đẳng lượng ngày càng xa nhau hơn.)

Returns to scale need not be uniform: A firm may exhibit IRS at low output (fixed costs of R&D spread, specialization benefits), CRS at medium output, and DRS at very high output (management diseconomies). Many real industries follow this pattern. (Trong thực tế: một công ty có thể trải qua IRS → CRS → DRS khi quy mô tăng dần.)

Test for returns to scale in q = K0.6L0.6: Replace K→tK, L→tL: q′ = (tK)0.6(tL)0.6 = t0.6K0.6 · t0.6L0.6 = t1.2·q. Since t1.2 > t (for t > 1), output increases by more than t when inputs scale by t → Increasing Returns to Scale. (q = K0.6L0.6: tổng số mũ = 0.6 + 0.6 = 1.2 > 1 → IRS. General rule for Cobb-Douglas q = KaLb: a + b > 1 → IRS; a + b = 1 → CRS; a + b < 1 → DRS.

⚠ #1 Exam Trap in Section 6 — DMR ≠ DRS

Diminishing Marginal Returns (DMR): Short run, one input (K) is fixed, adding labor eventually decreases MPL. Decreasing Returns to Scale (DRS): Long run, all inputs are scaled up proportionally, output increases by less than proportionally. These are completely separate concepts. A firm can simultaneously exhibit DMR to labor in the short run (K fixed) AND constant or increasing returns to scale in the long run (all inputs variable). Many exam questions test whether you can distinguish them. (DMR: ngắn hạn, K cố định, thêm L → MP giảm. DRS: dài hạn, tất cả tăng tỷ lệ, sản lượng tăng ít hơn tỷ lệ. HOÀN TOÀN KHÁC NHAU.)
